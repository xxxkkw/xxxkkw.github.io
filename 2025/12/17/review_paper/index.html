睡眠review

发表于2025-12-17|更新于2025-12-20
|评论数:

Summary

This paper proposes DSCF, a dual-path framework for EEG sleep staging that jointly optimizes a supervised classification path and a SimSiam-based contrastive path. A feature pyramid backbone extracts multiscale temporal features, a Temporal Context Encoder (TCE) with causal convolutions models temporal dependencies, and a stage-aware augmentation strategy (time-shift for N1/N2) drives the contrastive objective. Evaluated on Sleep-EDF-20, Sleep-EDF-78, and a 329-subject subset of SHHS, DSCF reports strong single- and multi-channel results and ablations indicating complementary gains from TCE and contrastive learning, particularly for ambiguous stages (N1, N2).

Strengths

Technical novelty and innovation

  • Combines a multiscale feature pyramid, a causal-conv Transformer-style TCE, and a SimSiam path into a single joint objective without pretraining, which is a clean and practically convenient design.

  • Introduces a stage-aware augmentation policy (targeting N1 and N2) to focus representation learning on confusable stages; shows empirical benefits.

  • Uses causal convolutions to instantiate Q/K/V in attention, which is a reasonable modification for time series.

Experimental rigor and validation

  • Evaluates on three public datasets with subject-wise splits and 20-fold cross-validation; reports multiple metrics (ACC, macro-F1, Kappa, per-class F1).

  • Ablations cover module contributions (backbone vs +TCE vs +contrastive), TCE depth, channel ablations, and alternative contrastive frameworks (SimCLR, BYOL, SimSiam).

  • Provides confusion matrices and t-SNE analyses to support claims about better separation of ambiguous classes.

  • The reported single-channel performance approaching that of multi-channel methods indicates strong robustness of the method.

  • The reported single-channel performance approaching that of multi-channel methods indicates strong robustness of the method, and the model achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) results across all three datasets (Sleep-EDF-20, Sleep-EDF-78, SHHS) in both single-channel and multi-channel configurations.

Weakness

  1. For example, on the Sleep-EDF-78 dataset, the confusion matrix reported in this manuscript shows a noticeably different error distribution compared with FlexibleSleepNet and DiffSleepNet. In particular, DiffSleepNet reports the REM row as [546, 1261, 1880, 21, 22627], with misclassifications mainly occurring in N2, whereas the proposed method reports [2064, 396, 507, 241, 22627], where errors are primarily shifted toward W. Similar DiffSleepNet-like patterns are also observed across other baseline methods. While such differences may arise from design choices or training strategies, the magnitude and consistency of this discrepancy across baselines warrant further clarification. A more detailed explanation of the factors contributing to this distinct confusion matrix structure would help readers better understand the behavior of the proposed model. In addition, providing per-fold confusion matrices along with a clear description of preprocessing and training settings would further enhance transparency and reproducibility.

  2. The training and evaluation pipeline is not described in sufficient detail, which limits reproducibility. In particular, the supervised loss and the contrastive loss are weighted equally, yet no justification or sensitivity analysis is provided. Given that the augmentation strategy is applied only to two specific classes, it would be helpful to include a hyperparameter study examining the loss weighting and to report how sensitive the performance is to these design choices.

  3. The choice of time-shift as the data augmentation strategy requires further justification. The manuscript does not clearly specify which invariances this augmentation is intended to enforce, nor why it is expected to yield substantial performance gains for sleep stage classification. A comparative ablation study against alternative augmentations—such as additive noise, temporal jitter, or frequency-domain perturbations—would strengthen the motivation and help isolate the contribution of time-shift.

  4. The implementation details of the SimSiam predictor are under-specified. The predictor architecture itself is not described, and the rationale for using stop-gradient or any separate-update strategy is not discussed. To improve clarity and reproducibility, the authors should explicitly describe the predictor design (e.g., number of layers, normalization, activation functions), the overall training configuration (projection head, predictor, and stop-gradient placement), and provide justification for the gradient handling strategy, supported by references or ablation results where appropriate.

  5. The design of the TCE module is insufficiently analyzed. The ablation study only considers stacking 0, 1, or 2 TCE layers, without providing theoretical motivation or empirical evidence for why two layers should be optimal. Deeper configurations are not explored, nor are potential issues such as overfitting or increased computational cost discussed. A more systematic analysis of depth versus performance and efficiency would strengthen this component.

  6. Important efficiency-related metrics are missing, including parameter counts, FLOPs, and training/inference time. Moreover, no efficiency comparison is provided against representative baselines such as FlexibleSleepNet or DiffSleepNet. As a result, it is unclear whether the reported performance gains arise from architectural improvements or simply from increased model capacity. Reporting results under matched or constrained parameter budgets would help clarify this point.
